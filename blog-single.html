How I Structured My Entire 1L Year Before It Started

Most students walk into 1L reactive. I walked in with a system. Here is the exact framework I used to plan my semester before classes began — and why it made all the difference.

Shakierah Smith
· May 12, 2025 · 8 min read

Law school does not reward the unprepared — but it does not have to catch you off guard, either. In the summer before my 1L year at the University at Buffalo School of Law, I made a decision that changed everything about how my first semester unfolded. I built a system before I ever set foot in a classroom.

This is not about over-preparing in a way that creates anxiety. It is about arriving with clarity on how you learn, how you will manage your time, and what you will do when things inevitably get hard. Here is exactly what I did — and what I recommend to every student I work with.

Step 1: Map the semester before it begins

The first thing I did was get every syllabus I could find for each of my 1L courses. Many professors post them publicly, or you can reach out to incoming students from the prior year. I built a simple calendar with every assignment, reading, and exam date in one place before orientation week.

This sounds basic, but most students do this reactively — week by week as assignments come due. Seeing the full semester in one view changed my relationship to stress. I could see what was coming. I could plan around it.

"Preparation is not about eliminating uncertainty. It is about reducing the surface area of surprise so that when something unexpected happens, you have the capacity to respond."

Step 2: Build your brief-writing workflow before 1L reading begins

Case briefs are the core unit of law school study. Most students develop their briefing system through trial and error — wasting weeks on formats that do not serve them in the Socratic classroom. I spent two weeks before school started writing practice briefs on cases I found online, experimenting with format, and arriving on day one with a system already dialed in.

My format was structured around five elements:

  • Facts — one to three sentences, only what matters for the legal question
  • Issue — the precise legal question the court is answering
  • Rule — the controlling legal principle
  • Analysis — how the court applied the rule to the facts
  • Holding — the decision, written in one sentence

That is it. Nothing more. I found that brevity served me better than comprehensiveness — because I was writing briefs I could actually use in class, not briefs that looked impressive on paper.

Step 3: Identify your support structure before you need it

Before classes started, I identified three things: where academic support resources lived, which professors held office hours and on what days, and whether any upper-level students were willing to answer questions. I did not wait until I was drowning to find a life raft.

Most students reach out for help after they have already fallen behind. By then, the semester has a momentum of its own. Finding your support structure in August means you arrive knowing how to use it in October.

The result

I graduated in the top five percent of my class. I am not sharing that to impress — I am sharing it because I want to be clear about what preparation produces. Not perfection. Not a stress-free experience. But a foundation solid enough to build on when things get hard, and they will get hard.

The Pinkprint Lawyer exists because this kind of guidance should not be a privilege. It should be available to every student willing to do the work — regardless of whether anyone in their family has walked this road before.

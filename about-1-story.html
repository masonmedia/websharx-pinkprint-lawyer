About Shakierah Smith

No roadmap. No family of attorneys. Just the will to figure it out.

A first-generation law student turned attorney — building the guidance she once had to find on her own.

01

My Story

Learning the rules in real time.

My path through law school was defined by preparation, discipline, and a deep commitment to excellence. I graduated in the top five percent of my class at the University at Buffalo School of Law with a cumulative 3.9 GPA, earning induction into the Order of the Coif and receiving multiple academic and service-based honors — including the Max Koren Award, the Monique E. Emdin Award, the Promise Prize Scholar Award from the Change Create Transform Foundation, and the John L. Hargrave Award from the Minority Bar Foundation.

None of it came with an instruction manual. I built my own, one semester at a time — and that experience is the foundation this platform is built on.

My Story — Chapter Two

Building what didn't exist yet.

I served as the inaugural Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Editor of the Buffalo Law Review — a role I helped create to promote inclusivity within the journal — and founded the First-Generation Law Students Association, providing structure and community for students navigating law school without a traditional roadmap.

Alongside my legal training, I spent years immersed in research, writing, and teaching-oriented roles: as a Faculty Research Scholar under Professor Guyora Binder, a Writing Fellow under Professor Kate Rowan, and through faculty assistantships with Professor Matthew Steilen, Professor Rebecca French, and Dean Gargano. My research contributions have been recognized in publications at the Columbia Law Review, Harvard Law & Policy Review, Emory Law Journal, and a leading criminal law casebook, and I co-authored a peer-reviewed article in the Criminal Justice Review.

I also participated in the Criminal Justice Advocacy Clinic at the University at Buffalo School of Law, which connected me with real clients in a meaningful way and deepened my understanding of the human impact of legal work.

Before Law School

The groundwork started at RIT.

Before law school, I completed a Master of Science in Criminal Justice and a Bachelor of Science in Criminal Justice and Communication at the Rochester Institute of Technology — graduating with a cumulative 4.0 GPA and ranking in the top 1% of the entire university.

I was selected as the College of Liberal Arts 2018 Undergraduate Commencement Speaker, participated in the McNair Scholars Program, the RIT Honors Program, and the Higher Education Opportunity Program (HEOP), and contributed to a nationally funded body-worn camera research project through the Bureau of Justice Assistance.

Practicing With Purpose, Today

New York City Partner & Co-Owner, Smith & Singleton Law.

Today, I practice law in real estate, immigration, business, and family law, and serve as the New York City Partner and Co-Owner of Smith & Singleton Law — a Black-owned law firm grounded in excellence, equity, and intentional advocacy.

The firm officially launched on May 17, 2026, intentionally marking the 72nd anniversary of the Supreme Court's decision in Brown v. Board of Education — a moment that continues to shape how we think about access, opportunity, and the law's role in social change.

My Mission

Preparation builds confidence.

Too often, legal education operates on silence and assumption — students are expected to already know the rules, the language, and the strategy.

When students understand how the system works — academically, professionally, and culturally — they show up differently and ask better questions.

I am committed to empowerment without gatekeeping — sharing information clearly, explaining the "why," and respecting that every path looks different.

This work isn't just about surviving the process — it's about understanding it, and moving through it with purpose.
